Adadelta

Adadelta is a more robust extension of Adagrad that adapts learning rates based on a moving window of gradient updates, instead of accumulating all past gradients. This way, Adadelta continues learning even when many updates have been done. Compared to Adagrad, in the original version of Adadelta you don't have to set an initial learning rate. In this version, initial learning rate and decay factor can be set, as in most other optimizers.

Adagrad

Adagrad is an optimizer with parameter-specific learning rates, which are adapted relative to how frequently a parameter gets updated during training. The more updates a parameter receives, the smaller the learning rate.

Adam

Adam is an optimization algorithm that can used instead of the classical stochastic gradient descent procedure to update network weights iterative based in training data.

Adam was presented by Diederik Kingma from OpenAI and Jimmy Ba from the University of Toronto in their 2015 ICLR paper(poster) titled “Adam: A Method for Stochastic Optimization“. I will quote liberally from their paper in this post, unless stated otherwise.

Adamax

Adamax optimizer from Adam paper's Section 7.

It is a variant of Adam based on the infinity norm.Default parameters follow those provided in the paper.

RMSProp

Nesterov Adam optimizer.

Much like Adam is essentially RMSprop with momentum, Nadam is Adam RMSprop with Nesterov momentum. Default parameters follow those provided in the paper.It is recommended to leave the parameters of this optimizer at their default values.

SGD

Stochastic gradient descent (often shortened to SGD), also known as incremental gradient descent, is an iterative method for optimizing a differentiable objective function, a stochastic approximation of gradient descent optimization. A 2018 article[1] implicitly credits Herbert Robbins and Sutton Monro for developing SGD in their 1951 article titled "A Stochastic Approximation Method"; see Stochastic approximation for more information. It is called stochastic because samples are selected randomly (or shuffled) instead of as a single group (as in standard gradient descent) or in the order they appear in the training set.
